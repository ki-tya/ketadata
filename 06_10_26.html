The split matters: the inflation surge is almost entirely a war story, not a broad economic overheating story. Markets largely expect the Fed to hold at June 18 FOMC. Stock futures held negative but came off their lows after the print. Treasury yields flat.

The Iran "pay the price" language alongside a 4.2% headline CPI print is the complete picture of the pressure the administration is under to close the war: economic data is now screaming for resolution, and public rhetoric is escalating to try to force it.

CORE VS HEADLINE
The 130 basis point spread between headline CPI (4.2%) and core CPI (2.9%) is the most important number in this morning's release. It means almost all of the inflation problem is energy — which means it is almost entirely a war problem, not a demand overheating problem. The Fed cannot fix a war with interest rates. A rate hike would suppress demand without touching the supply-side energy inflation. The core reading below estimate gives the Fed cover to hold at June 18. The headline number gives politicians cover to keep pressure on Iran. These two readings are being instrumentalized for different purposes simultaneously.

FED JUNE 18 — NOW CLEARER

The question for the statement language is whether they acknowledge the war explicitly or continue to use the phrase "supply chain disruptions" as a euphemism for the conflict they cannot say they are waiting to resolve.
