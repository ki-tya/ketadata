Hezbollah rejects US-brokered Israel-Lebanon ceasefire — Iran says no tangible progress in talks. The US announced Wednesday that Israel and Lebanon had agreed to a conditional ceasefire — contingent on Hezbollah ceasing fire and withdrawing from southern Lebanon. Hezbollah rejected the terms outright. Iran's FM Araghchi said on Thursday that "no tangible progress" has been made in negotiations and warned that any Israeli strike on Beirut would trigger full-scale resumption of the US-Iran conflict. Israel continues strikes across Lebanon. IDF is ordering forced displacement of at least nine towns in southern Lebanon. Iran fired missile and drone "warning shots" at US warships in the Gulf of Oman, accusing the US of harassing marine traffic.

May jobs report this morning — the Fed pivot hinge. Economists expect 80,000 jobs added in May, well below the previous months' pace. Unemployment expected to hold at 4.3%. The number will shape Fed policy expectations going into the June 18 FOMC meeting. S&P 500 is attempting its 10th consecutive positive week — longest potential winning streak since 1985. Stock futures are down this morning ahead of the report.

Crypto continues descent — five reasons running simultaneously. BTC opened at $63,812 Friday, trading to $62,411 by 7:29am ET. ETH opened at $1,768, fell to $1,676. Both down steadily all week. The five drivers: Iran war inflation pressure, Fed hawkish pivot, Broadcom-led semiconductor selloff, risk-off rotation, sustained ETF outflows. Bitcoin has now erased all gains made since the war began February 28.

Broadcom dragged Nasdaq Thursday — AI chip narrative under pressure. Semiconductor and AI-related shares fell after Broadcom's earnings. Dow hit a record Thursday while Nasdaq finished lower — the split showing a rotation out of growth/tech into more defensive industrial and financial names. S&P closed at 7,584 Thursday (+0.41%), recovering Wednesday's decline.

Ebola — Kenya standoff ongoing. Court suspension of US quarantine facility still active. No confirmed European fallback. DRC border deteriorating. WHO PHEIC active.

OPEN QUESTIONS
THE HEZBOLLAH VARIABLE
The US-brokered deal required Hezbollah to agree. Hezbollah rejected it. This is the same structural problem that has locked the deal for weeks — Iran requires Lebanon enforcement, Netanyahu has electoral reasons to keep striking, and Hezbollah has its own strategic calculus that doesn't answer to either Washington or Tehran fully. The ceasefire announced Wednesday is already dead. The question is what the next iteration looks like and who initiates it.
JOBS REPORT + FED
80,000 jobs expected. If the number comes in hot, inflation concerns resurface and the Fed hike scenario gets priced more aggressively — bad for crypto, bad for growth stocks, bad for the rate-sensitive housing market. If it comes in soft or negative, recession concerns surface instead. There's a narrow corridor where markets can interpret the number as Goldilocks. The Fed June 18 meeting is two weeks out. This number sets the terms of that meeting.

  • Coverage consistently frames the Israel-Lebanon situation as a US-Iran-Israel three-party problem. Hezbollah is treated as a proxy responding to Tehran's instructions. But Hezbollah rejected the ceasefire deal despite Iranian diplomatic positioning — which suggests Hezbollah is operating its own strategic calculus, not purely executing Iranian orders. That agreement requires different negotiations than what's currently happening.
