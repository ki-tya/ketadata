THE SPLIT MARKET
S&P 500 and Nasdaq are at record highs while Russell 2000 is down 0.89% and under 39% of US stocks are actually going up today. The rally is being carried by a small number of large-cap tech companies and energy stocks. This is a narrow rally, not a broad healthy one. Small caps are more sensitive to interest rates — they depend on floating-rate debt and benefit most from rate cuts. The PCE reading and oil inflation are making cuts less likely, which hits small caps while mega-cap tech is largely insulated.
WHY OIL IS UP TODAY
Iran suspended peace talks this morning in response to Israel's expanding Lebanon operations. The Strait of Hormuz — which carries roughly 20% of global oil supply — has been effectively closed since the war began on February 28. It is now day 93. Every day the strait stays closed, supply tightens further. The IRGC is also threatening to open the Bab el-Mandeb strait as a second front — a threat that, if real, would compound Hormuz by closing the Red Sea's southern entrance simultaneously. JPMorgan had projected $97/barrel oil if Hormuz reopened in June. That reopening is now deferred.
THE GOLD PARADOX
Gold is supposed to go up when the world is unstable. Today it's going down. The mechanism: oil spike → inflation fears rise → markets expect the Fed to delay rate cuts → higher real yields make gold (which pays no interest) less attractive → gold falls. Gold peaked at $5,600 in January 2026 when the war began. It has corrected to $4,527. The same war that drove the peak is now, through the inflation pathway, suppressing it. This is one of the cleaner demonstrations of how macro mechanics override the safe-haven narrative in the short term.
BITCOIN AS TECH PROXY
Bitcoin is supposed to be "digital gold" — an uncorrelated alternative store of value. In practice it has an 85.4% correlation with the Nasdaq-100 during oil price spikes. When oil goes up, risk sentiment drops, tech sells off, and Bitcoin sells off with it. The institutional money that entered Bitcoin through spot ETFs — which were supposed to be long-term strategic holders — is now leaving at a record pace: longest outflow streak on record, including a $1.2 billion position exit. The safe-haven thesis is not holding in the current macro environment.
IRAN SUSPENSION — WHAT CHANGED
Friday: Trump entered Situation Room for "final determination," emerged with no decision and toughened terms. Saturday: Iran given days to respond to new framework. Sunday: nothing. Monday: Iran suspended talks entirely, citing Israel's Lebanon operations as a ceasefire precondition that has been violated. The deal that markets were pricing optimism into for two weeks is now in reverse. This is the third time in three weeks that a deal was described as imminent before additional conditions emerged. Each iteration has been covered as a new event rather than as a pattern.
DIMON'S WARNING IN CONTEXT
The S&P 500 has posted nine consecutive weeks of gains. It climbed through Iran war, ceasefire violations, mutual strikes, record inflation readings, a failed Situation Room meeting, and now a talks suspension. Jamie Dimon — whose risk management credibility is arguably higher than any other institutional voice — said last Friday markets are "exuberant" and risks are underpriced. The question he's raising is not whether the market will fall today. It's whether the accumulated geopolitical and macro risk is correctly priced into assets that are sitting at all-time highs. The market's answer so far is: yes. His answer is: no.
THE BAB EL-MANDEB THREAT
This is the most underanalyzed item in today's news cycle. Bab el-Mandeb is the strait between Yemen and Djibouti — the southern entrance to the Red Sea, and thus to the Suez Canal. Houthi attacks have already forced significant container traffic to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10–14 days to Asia-Europe transit. If Iran directly operationalises Bab el-Mandeb alongside Hormuz, two of the world's five major maritime chokepoints would be simultaneously closed. The compounded supply chain effect — energy, consumer goods, shipping costs — is not currently being calculated in available market analysis. Oil is up $2.50 today. If the threat becomes operational, the move would be significantly larger.
