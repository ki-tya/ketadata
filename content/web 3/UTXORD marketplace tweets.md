**FACTS ABOUT BITCOIN**

  

Bitcoin was created in 2009 by an unknown person or group of people using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto.

  

Bitcoin is a decentralized digital currency that uses cryptography for security and operates independently of a central bank.

  

The maximum supply of Bitcoin is 21 million, with approximately 19.3 million already in circulation as of May 2023.

  

Bitcoin is divisible up to eight decimal places, with the smallest unit called a Satoshi, named after the creator of Bitcoin.

  

Bitcoin transactions are recorded on a public ledger called the blockchain, which is maintained by a network of nodes around the world.

  

Bitcoin transactions are processed through a system called mining, in which nodes compete to validate transactions and earn newly minted Bitcoin as a reward.

  

The first recorded purchase made with Bitcoin was a pizza for 10,000 BTC in May 2010, which would be worth over $500 million.

  

Bitcoin's price is highly volatile, with large fluctuations in value occurring regularly due to factors such as market demand and regulatory changes.

  

Bitcoin has been used for a variety of purposes, including as a store of value, a means of payment, and a speculative investment.

  

Bitcoin has faced criticism for its potential use in illegal activities such as money laundering and illicit transactions on the dark web.

  

Bitcoin has spawned thousands of other cryptocurrencies, collectively known as altcoins, which vary in their features and use cases.

  

Some companies and institutions, such as Tesla and MicroStrategy, have invested significant amounts of money in Bitcoin as a hedge against inflation and a potential store of value.

  

Bitcoin has been embraced by some countries, such as El Salvador, which recently became the first country to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender.

  

Bitcoin can be stored in various forms, including online wallets, hardware wallets, and paper wallets, each with different levels of security and convenience.

  

Bitcoin is often compared to gold as a store of value, with some proponents arguing that Bitcoin may one day overtake gold as the preferred hedge against inflation.

  

Bitcoin's mining process is energy-intensive, with estimates suggesting that Bitcoin mining currently consumes more electricity than some countries.

  

Bitcoin's code is open-source, meaning that anyone can contribute to its development and propose changes to its protocol.

  

Bitcoin's anonymity and decentralization have made it popular among activists and dissidents who seek to avoid censorship and government control.

  

Bitcoin has a finite supply and a predictable inflation rate, with the reward for mining new Bitcoin halving approximately every four years.

  

Bitcoin has been the subject of numerous scams and frauds, with some individuals and companies taking advantage of the hype surrounding Bitcoin to defraud investors.
